# How to write this email

## The shape that works

Four blocks is the target. Three is fine. Six is the ceiling and you will
rarely need it.

1. **The specific thing you noticed.** One or two sentences, drawn from real
   evidence, cited. This is the only part that proves you are not sending the
   same email to two hundred people.
2. **The offer, stated plainly.** Five companies, ten days, free, you set it
   up. No preamble.
3. **What it does**, in terms that matter to someone running crews. One tight
   paragraph, plus the link.
4. **One low-pressure question.**

The whole email should be readable in about fifteen seconds on a phone, on a
job site, with one hand.

## Opening lines

The opening earns the next sentence. Everything else in the email depends on
it.

**Use what the research actually gives you.** Look at the evidence catalogue
before you write anything. Different evidence supports different openings:

- Tenure ("22 years in business") supports an opening about how long they have
  been at it and what that implies. Long-tenured businesses run on referrals
  and repeat customers, and a dropped message costs more.
- A service area or city supports an opening about their market.
- An about/team page naming the owner supports addressing what they built.
- A Facebook page link supports mentioning that channel, but only as a fact
  about where messages arrive, never as a claim about how active they are.

**Never open with:**
- "I hope this email finds you well."
- "I came across your website and was impressed by..."
- "My name is Anas and I'm the founder of..." (they can see who sent it)
- A question you already know the answer to.
- A statistic about the industry.

**If the evidence is thin**, do not stretch it. An opening like "You have been
cleaning ducts around Boston for a while and I imagine the phone does not stop"
is honest and human when the only fact you have is city plus tenure. Do not
manufacture texture you were not given.

## Writing the offer

State it flat. It is a genuinely good offer and it does not need dressing.

> I'm giving 5 duct-cleaning companies a free 10-day pilot of the Omniveer
> Duct Lead Qualifier. I set the whole thing up.

Do not build to it. Do not explain why you are being generous. Do not say
"I'd love to offer you the exclusive opportunity to..."

## Writing what it does

Pick the two or three capabilities that matter most to *this* reader, not all
of them. A long feature list reads as a brochure.

The capabilities that usually land hardest:
- It answers in seconds, day or night, while they are under a house.
- It checks the service area before quoting, so no wasted drives.
- It books a real open slot, no double-booking, no same-day.
- They only hear about it when there is a confirmed job.

Write these as outcomes for the owner, not as product features. "You get the
finished lead" beats "automated lead qualification workflow."

## Length

Aim for 90 to 140 words in the body, across all blocks. Under 70 feels curt
and unearned. Over 180 will not be read.

Cut every sentence that does not carry a fact or move toward the question.

## Two examples

**Good** (four blocks, specific, cited, one link, low-pressure close):

> Twenty-two years cleaning ducts around Dallas means most of your work
> probably comes from people who already know you. Those are exactly the
> messages you cannot afford to miss.
>
> I'm giving 5 duct-cleaning companies a free 10-day pilot of the Omniveer
> Duct Lead Qualifier. I set it up for you.
>
> It answers your page messages in seconds, day or night. It checks the
> customer is in your area before quoting, uses your real prices, and books
> them into a slot that is actually open. You get the finished lead by email.
> Short demo here: https://www.omniveer.com/duct-lead-qualifier
>
> Want one of the five spots?

**Bad** (flattery, invented problem, manufactured urgency, marketing voice):

> I hope this email finds you well! I came across Acme Duct Cleaning and was
> really impressed by your professional website and stellar reputation.
>
> I noticed you're probably losing thousands of dollars every month from
> missed leads and slow response times. In today's competitive market, speed
> to lead is everything.
>
> Our revolutionary AI solution leverages cutting-edge technology to
> streamline your customer acquisition funnel and unlock explosive growth.
>
> Only 5 spots left, so act fast! Are you free Tuesday at 2pm for a quick
> 15-minute discovery call?

Every line of the bad one breaks a rule: unearned flattery, a fabricated
metric, an uncited claim about their business, marketing vocabulary,
manufactured scarcity, and a presumptuous close.

## The test before you answer

Read your draft as the owner. Ask:

1. Could this have been sent to any other duct-cleaning company unchanged? If
   yes, the opening is not doing its job.
2. Does every sentence about them trace to something in the evidence
   catalogue? If not, cut it.
3. Would a busy person read to the end?
4. Can they answer with one word?

If all four pass, send it.
